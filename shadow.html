The Iron Shadow

“I remember it like it was yesterday, the day that thing was born. We had finally ended the war with ‘em Clodaks after just ‘bout month of fighting. We won o’course, but not without our fair share of the losses. As we was walking back to camp, I gave one last glance o’er my shoulder, to say my goodbyes n’ such. And that’s when I saw ‘im. It was Emory. Now I was straight frazzled ‘cuz I swear I done seen him get run through not but thirty minutes ago. But I just reckon he got mighty lucky with where that rapier gone through, so I told my conroi we done left one behind and hauled ass to go bring ‘im home.”

“But that wasn’ Emory, a’least not no more. He had that look. The look I was dreadin’ seein’ on his wife’s face. That haunted empty look. ‘n no matter how much we called, he just stayed there with that same look upon ‘is face. It was like ‘e was a ghost or summin’.”

“’n then the strangest thing happened. If there wasn’t the rest of my conroi who done seen it too, y’all woulda put me in the loonie bin and said I was off my rocker. The ghost, or whatever I’was, walked over to another fallen Joe and sudd’nly his ghost was there too. An’ ‘e kept on goin’ from man to man, ‘til there were hundreds of them. Both our men and the Clodaks. They was still wearing their broken armour and wielding their broken weapons. All with that same damn look on their faces. ‘n then, just as soon as they came, they was gone, leaving behind this hunk o’ scrap. It din’t have no eyes or mouth, but I swear I done saw the same haunted look on it too. But we din’t know what to do with it, so we went back to camp for the night. It wasn’ even first dawn before we returned. But by then the scrap had upped and disappeared.”

-Old Man Willard
